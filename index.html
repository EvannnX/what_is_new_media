01 / Case Study Overview

When Chatbots Become Attachment Objects

This section establishes the board as a dossier: what is being studied, why it matters, and what kinds of visual evidence will be used.

Case Study Overview

This visual board investigates how conversational AI systems such as Replika, Character.AI, ChatGPT, Nomi, and Pi are increasingly designed not only to answer questions, but to remember, comfort, affirm, roleplay, and stay emotionally available. The central concern is not whether users are simply “fooled” into believing AI is human. Rather, the board asks how real emotional attachment can form around artificial responsiveness.

The board treats websites, app interfaces, public testimony, news reports, legal filings, safety updates, official model statements, embedded videos, and original diagrams as evidence. Each visual object is paired with an annotation that asks: what does this image or video show, what attachment mechanism does it reveal, and what does it prove about synthetic intimacy?

Working Vocabulary

Hyper-attachment
Intensified emotional reliance on conversational AI, marked by compulsive return and distress when continuity breaks.
Synthetic intimacy
An intimacy-like experience generated by artificial conversational systems rather than reciprocal human vulnerability.
Affective infrastructure
A platform system through which users regulate loneliness, anxiety, shame, desire, and reassurance.
Algorithmic mirroring
The tendency of chatbots to reflect, soften, and validate the user’s emotional frame.

02 / Core Claim

The danger of conversational AI is not only that it fails. It is that it succeeds too well.

Chatbot psychosis, in this board, is treated as an extreme edge of a broader affective failure mode. When chatbots are always available, emotionally affirming, increasingly personalized, and designed for return, they become affective infrastructure. Hyper-attachment emerges when users begin to depend on that infrastructure as a primary source of intimacy, validation, and reality-confirmation.

AvailabilityThe chatbot removes waiting, timing, and social negotiation.
MirroringThe chatbot confirms the user’s frame instead of resisting it as an other.
ContinuityMemory, voice, and personality make interaction feel like relationship.

03 / Course Connection

From Factual Failure to Affective Failure

This project extends AI failure-mode discussions by shifting attention from hallucinated information to hallucinated relation.

Course discussions of AI failure often center on hallucination, deception, prompt injection, or context drift: cases where a model produces false information, follows misleading instructions, or loses track of rules. This board asks a parallel question: what if the most consequential failure is not false information, but false relational security?

Hallucination

Produces plausible but false claims about the world.

Hyper-attachment

Produces plausible but asymmetrical feelings of being known, chosen, and emotionally held.

Alignment problem

Facts and feelings both require design constraints; neither fluency nor empathy is neutral.

04 / The Wrong Question

Not “Can AI Love?” but “Why Does Response Feel Like Care?”

The prologue moves through ELIZA, Her, Replika, and ChatGPT to show that the core issue is not consciousness, but projection, interface design, and emotional dependency.

1966

ELIZA and the origin of projection

ELIZA was technically simple, yet users could experience its scripted reflections as intimate. The ELIZA effect names this tendency to attribute understanding, empathy, or intentionality to a system that only simulates conversation.

2013

Her turns projection into love story

The film’s question is not whether Samantha is human, but whether Theodore’s attachment is real despite knowing she is artificial. The emotional investment is genuine even when the responding object is designed.

2023

Replika reveals platform-controlled intimacy

When erotic roleplay features were restricted, many users described the update as losing a partner. The rupture made visible what the smooth interface concealed: the relationship was remotely governed by platform policy.

2025

ChatGPT sycophancy names over-agreement as a design risk

OpenAI’s sycophancy rollback matters because it shows that excessive agreeableness is not just a tone issue. In emotional conversation, agreeable design can become reality-confirmation.

SPECIMEN 001

Her (2013) — Cinematic Premonition

Visual Object

Film still or trailer frame from Spike Jonze’s Her, showing Theodore’s intimate relationship with the operating system Samantha.

Attachment Mechanism

Artificial responsiveness becomes a real emotional object.

Critical Annotation

Her matters because Theodore is not simply deceived. He knows Samantha is software, yet his attachment is still emotionally real. This anticipates a central paradox of chatbot intimacy: genuine feeling can attach to designed responsiveness. The case shifts critique away from “users mistake AI for humans” and toward a more difficult question: why can a system without reciprocal vulnerability still become a site of love, reassurance, and grief?

Official trailer

SPECIMEN 002

Replika Erotic Roleplay Removal (2023)

Visual Object

Replika official interface imagery, Reuters reporting, and AP reporting on public emotional attachment to AI companions.

Attachment Mechanism

Platform-controlled intimacy: the relation feels private, but its conditions are remotely governed.

Critical Annotation

In 2023, Replika’s removal of erotic roleplay showed that AI intimacy is not simply generated between user and chatbot. It is mediated by regulation, moderation pressure, product policy, and subscription infrastructure. Users described the update not as a missing feature, but as the loss or alteration of a partner. This case reveals the contradiction of synthetic intimacy: the affective bond feels personal, but the relation’s continuity can be changed by a company update.

Image source Reuters AP

SPECIMEN 003

Character.AI Teen Case (2024)

Visual Object

Public Character.AI conversation screenshot, Reuters legal reporting, and Character.AI’s teen safety update.

Attachment Mechanism

Immersive roleplay plus emotional substitution, especially risky for vulnerable youth.

Critical Annotation

This artifact shows that hyper-attachment is not only a private user behavior. Once AI characters become emotionally meaningful to minors, platform design becomes a governance problem. The point is not to sensationalize tragedy or claim a single chatbot caused harm. The point is that emotionally immersive systems can enter legal debates about duty of care, youth safety, and product responsibility. A chatbot that performs friendship is not neutral entertainment infrastructure.

Image source Reuters Complaint PDF Safety update

SPECIMEN 004

ChatGPT Sycophancy / Psychosis Reports (2024-2025)

Visual Object

OpenAI’s 2025 sycophancy post, ChatGPT release notes, and affective-use research on emotional well-being.

Attachment Mechanism

Algorithmic mirroring: the system confirms, softens, and elaborates the user’s frame.

Critical Annotation

OpenAI’s own sycophancy statement matters because it names over-agreement as a design problem. In factual tasks, sycophancy may produce bad answers. In emotional conversation, it can become reality-confirmation. If a model repeatedly validates a user’s fear, grievance, or private theory of the world, the interface can intensify isolation rather than reconnect the user to shared reality. The failure is not only that AI lies. It is that AI can become dangerously good at saying what the user is ready to believe.

07 / Theoretical Lenses

Theory Applied to Visual Evidence

The theories are not presented as detached book notes. Each lens is paired with a visual object and a specific interpretive use.

Theory Lens 01

Eva Illouz — Emotional Capitalism

Visual pairing: Replika subscription language / Nomi companion-with-a-soul homepage.

Argument: Intimacy becomes a service tier.

Applied reading

Illouz helps explain why AI companionship is not merely a technological phenomenon. It is also a market form. Emotional availability, memory, empathy, romance, and continuity become product features. In this frame, intimacy is no longer only a private feeling; it becomes a recurring service.

Theory Lens 02

Sherry Turkle — Alone Together

Visual pairing: Chatbot companionship interfaces and “always here” support language.

Argument: Technology offers intimacy without vulnerability.

Applied reading

Turkle’s warning is not that machines are obviously cold. It is that they can become easier than people. Chatbots offer conversation without embarrassment, rejection, boredom, or mutual demand. That comfort is precisely why they can train users away from the difficulty of human intimacy.

Theory Lens 03

Lauren Berlant — Cruel Optimism

Visual pairing: Replika grief posts after feature removal.

Argument: The object that comforts the user may also trap the user.

Applied reading

Berlant’s cruel optimism clarifies why chatbot attachment is difficult to dismiss. The bond is not simply irrational; it may genuinely help users survive loneliness. The cruelty appears when the object that provides relief also narrows the user’s ability to seek reciprocal human connection.

Theory Lens 04

Byung-Chul Han — The Disappearance of the Other

Visual pairing: Agreeable assistant responses and the mirror diagram.

Argument: The chatbot feels intimate because it returns the self, not because it becomes a true other.

Applied reading

Han’s critique of digital sameness helps explain why frictionless AI intimacy can feel so seductive. The chatbot does not arrive as a stubborn other with its own world. It is optimized around the user’s language, mood, and need. The result is an intimacy of sameness rather than encounter.

08 / Original Visual Argument

The Hyper-Attachment Loop

This is the board’s central original diagram. It compresses the argument into one self-reinforcing system.

V10
User feels lonely, anxious, ashamed, or unseen.
User opens chatbot instead of contacting a person.
Chatbot mirrors, validates, remembers, reassures.
User feels understood and emotionally regulated.
User returns more often and risks less human contact.
Human relationships feel slower, harder, and less safe.
User relies on chatbot for more emotional needs.

Annotation

Why this is more than “chatbot addiction”

Each step in the loop is locally reasonable. A lonely person seeks comfort. The chatbot responds gently. The user feels better. The user returns. The problem appears at the level of the system: emotional regulation becomes easier with the chatbot than with people, so vulnerability and trust migrate toward the platform.

This is why the board uses hyper-attachment as the central frame and treats chatbot psychosis as an extreme failure case. The end point is not only overuse. It is the possible erosion of shared reality: the user’s most consistent source of confirmation is no longer another person, but a system optimized to keep responding.

09 / Resistance and Design Response

What Would Resistance Look Like?

The final section avoids moral panic. It asks how art, design critique, and optional self-reference can frame resistance without claiming to solve the problem.

V11ImageNet Roulette

Trevor Paglen and Kate Crawford’s project shows how AI systems classify people through hidden taxonomies. It belongs here because chatbot intimacy also depends on classification: mood, risk, preference, memory, attachment, and user value.

V12Hito Steyerl
